Insights · Oakleaf at 21

By Richard Colgan, Group Founder & CEO  |  4 July 2026

As we celebrate the 21st anniversary of Oakleaf Partnership — and my 26th year working in partnership with the HR profession — I want to thank all the clients, candidates and friends who have helped us along on this incredible journey.

Twenty-one years ago, 4th July 2005 was my own personal “Independence Day”. As an entrepreneur, I soon realised that the motto of my alma mater, the Royal Military Academy Sandhurst — “Serve to Lead” — could not be more relevant: with the power to shape your own destiny comes great responsibility, and no less so than now, as we face an extremely challenging global economic and geopolitical environment. A healthy dose of resilience has proved quite useful, too.

A profession under more pressure than ever

As I reflect on the ups and downs of the last 26 years, I see a profession that feels under more pressure than I have ever witnessed. Recent data from the Ultimate Resilience HR Wellbeing Report and Stribe’s Big HR Check-in reveals a profession under severe strain in the UK.

HR seems caught in the middle — squeezed by executive demands, myriad compliance complexities and heightened employee expectations. The post-Covid bow wave continues to wash over us. Is this a profession that, as has happened before, still can’t quite bring itself to say: “enough is enough”?

AI: real gains, and new problems

According to Gemini (ahem), generative AI has driven a 25% increase in HR department productivity. What it has also created, however, are legal, regulatory and ethical concerns that worry almost everyone I talk to. For many, the time savings are outweighed by the exponential growth of perfectly (AI-)crafted grievances — not to mention the rise of subject access requests (SARs). Contemporary research is clear that investing in technology alone is not enough.

Sustainable competitive advantage depends entirely on coupling AI with psychological safety, strategic upskilling and a genuinely human-centric culture.

This is the heart of the current disconnect. Many HR functions remain both under-resourced and under-skilled for this new paradigm. The need for “Enterprise HR leaders” is 100% desirable as a concept and an aspiration — but I’m not certain many HR leaders currently have the bandwidth to look beyond the end of the week. The void between expectation and reality has never been more startling.

A changing recruitment landscape

The recruitment landscape is undergoing rapid, AI-driven change. The current theme of “skills-based recruitment” — heavily driven by LinkedIn over the last decade — promises to remove human bias and discrimination (check out Mobley vs Workday). And yet AI screeners have shown a 67–82% preference bias towards AI-generated CVs. In the rapidly evolving world we live in, surely qualities like learning agility, resilience, potential and — dare I say it — culture fit are equally vital? “Easy Apply” is now being termed “Easy Reject” by many jobseekers (hence the growth of SARs). When did people stop caring about their employer brand?

How Oakleaf has evolved

Of course, Oakleaf has evolved over the years too, and continues to do so, to meet the environment we find ourselves in.

We are not, and never have been, transactional “order takers”.

We have deep, long-lasting relationships and never differentiate between clients and candidates — both are customers to us. Our network, and our holistic view of the market and the profession, is our enduring strength. We offer true global, confidential consulting and rapid solutions for our customers — always have, and always will.

My takeaways

Where the profession stands today

  • There is a severe disconnect between expectation and reality. Are HR leaders challenging C-suite expectations strongly enough? Incoming CIPD boss Neil Carberry notes that almost half of UK organisations remain in the experimental stage of AI adoption — “a huge opportunity for us as professionals”. An opportunity, yes — but an opportunity cost, too.
  • HR functions seem universally under-resourced and, in places, short of evolving skills (even if most CVs now imply everyone is an AI “black belt”). Recent CIPD research suggests 58% of HR teams currently prioritise short-term cost management.
  • The relationship between employees and employers has fundamentally changed post-Covid, and expectations have risen. The need for HR has, arguably, never been greater.
  • Individual resilience is on the wane — researchers call it an “Emotional Recession”.
  • A function that is “always on” is not sustainable.

Reason for optimism

I do, however, remain hugely optimistic about the opportunity the profession now has to finally secure its — well-deserved — seat at the top table.

It’s all about moral courage.

Happy Independence Day to you all — particularly our customers, and my wonderful Oakleaf team in the US.
